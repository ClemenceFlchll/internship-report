1 State of art

1.1 Differents kind of antennas

Antenna design and fabrication face numerous challenges. One ideal antenna must be small, low cost and with high performances, which englobes great directivity, fast reactivity, MIMO (multiple input, multiple output, ie making several beams at a time) and an ability to be used in different frequencies.

As for today, different antenna co-exist for different uses. For example we have the Dipole antenna, a simple and common antenna which is omnidirectional and is widely used in radio communication systems, television, and other wireless transmission applications. There's also the Yagi antenna, or rake antenna, which consists of several source points. It's like an antenna array whose elements are fed by mutual induction. If strand spacing and length are optimal, the radiation pattern and electronic gain is that of an array.



But when it comes to more complex systems such as radar, satellite communication systems, and 5G networks, only two kinds of antenna seems to stand out :


  • Phased-array antenna : One of the major problems with antennas is their directivity, i.e. their ability to direct a wave in a given direction, without too much loss in the other directions, the so-called "side lobes". In 1946, Antony Hewish and Martin Ryle developed a solution to this problem that doesn't require moving the antenna. By using several antenna arrays, each of which is phase-controlled, it is possible to produce a wavefront of any directivity. This allows to improve the gain, and enable advanced functionalities like beamforming. However, phased-array antenna with its inherent structure using several antennas, is necessarily expensive in terms of energy and materials, which can drive up to cost of fabrication and thus the antenna cost.

  • Parabolic Antenna They are primarily used for satellite communications and satellite television and are highly directional and offer very high gain, focusing signals onto a specific focal point. The corresponding faults are that directivity is good in a single direction, predefined by the antenna orientation. They are useless for "on the move" use, for example.



In today's satcom and microwave applications, phased array antennas are everywhere. They are the most efficient, despite their high connectivity costs, since each small antenna must be fed independently. The Greenerwave antenna must therefore retain the advantages of the phased array antenna, while overcoming these shortcomings.
